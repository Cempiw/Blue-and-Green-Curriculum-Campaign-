Understanding the Curricula

Two Movements,
One Mission for Earth

Both the Blue Curriculum and the Greening Curriculum are UNESCO-backed frameworks that embed environmental understanding into formal education — preparing young people to become informed, active planetary citizens.

🌊 IOC-UNESCO · 2022 · Ocean Literacy

Blue Curriculum

Developed by IOC-UNESCO (2022) within the UN Decade of Ocean Science 2021–2030, the Blue Curriculum is a toolkit for policymakers to integrate Ocean Literacy into national school curricula. It recognizes that the ocean is Earth's life-support system — and that future citizens must deeply understand it.

Knowledge and Awareness of Ocean Systems
Nature-Connectedness with the Marine World
Values and Attitudes Toward Ocean Sustainability
Competences for Sustainable Ocean Action
Cross-Disciplinary and Whole-School Approach
🌿 UNESCO GEP · 2024 · Climate Education

Greening Curriculum

A 2024 UNESCO guidance framework to integrate climate change and sustainability into all subjects across all age groups — from age 5 to 18 and beyond. It sets clear learning outcomes per age group. Developed with 224 global organizations, it targets 90% of countries integrating climate into curricula by 2030.

Cognitive, Social and Emotional Climate Learning
Action-Oriented and Justice-Driven Approach
Cross-Curricular Climate Integration
Learner-Centred and Experiential Pedagogy
Community-Based and Local-Knowledge Rooted
Why It Matters

The Urgent Case
for Change

Despite the ocean covering 70% of Earth and climate change threatening every ecosystem, environmental literacy is still absent from most national school curricula worldwide.

🌊

Ocean Illiteracy is Widespread

Ocean-related educational content is nearly absent from school curricula globally, even though the ocean provides food for over 1 billion people and regulates Earth's climate systems.

🌡️

Youth Demand Climate Education

Young people at the 2022 UN Transforming Education Summit called urgently for prioritizing sustainability education — especially climate education — to build resilience and ensure climate justice.

📚

Curricula Must Evolve

National education systems often fail to equip learners to face the climate crisis effectively. Clear benchmarks for empowering individuals as agents of change are urgently needed.

🤝

Education Drives Behaviour

Ocean Literacy directly catalyzes action to protect, conserve, and sustainably use the ocean. Education is the most powerful long-term tool to transform behaviour at a global scale.

🌐

Linked to Global Goals

Both curricula directly support SDG 4 (Quality Education), SDG 13 (Climate Action), SDG 14 (Life Below Water), and SDG 15 (Life on Land) from the UN Agenda 2030.

🇮🇩

Especially Vital for Indonesia

As the world's largest archipelagic nation with the second-longest coastline and the heart of the Coral Triangle, Indonesia has a unique urgency to integrate ocean and climate literacy into education.

Real-World Cases

Around the World

Countries and regions are already implementing Blue and Green Curriculum frameworks — proving that these ideas work across diverse cultural and geographic contexts.

🇵🇹
Blue Curriculum

Portugal — National Ocean Literacy Integration

Portugal became a leading example by embedding ocean content across multiple school subjects, supported directly by the Ministry of the Sea. Local coastal culture was leveraged to connect students with marine ecosystems and sustainable fisheries in a deeply contextual way.

🇰🇪
Blue Curriculum

Kenya — Ocean Literacy Beyond the Coast

Kenya's case demonstrated that ocean literacy must reach inland students too — overcoming "terrestrial bias" by connecting the national economy, fishing communities, and Swahili coastal heritage to school curricula across all regions of the country.

🇨🇷
Blue Curriculum

Costa Rica — Biodiversity and Blue Economy

Costa Rica integrated ocean content with its renowned biodiversity education, linking marine protected areas, sustainable tourism, and coral reef science into environmental and science subjects from primary school onward as a comprehensive national strategy.

🇧🇷
Blue Curriculum

Brazil — Where Amazon Meets the Atlantic

Brazil's case showed how river literacy can bridge into ocean literacy — connecting the Amazon basin, freshwater ecosystems, and Atlantic marine biodiversity into one unified environmental education narrative that spans the country's entire geography.

🇮🇳
Green Curriculum

India — Climate Education at National Scale

India's Ministry of Education, together with the National Council of Educational Research and Training (NCERT), participated in piloting the Greening Curriculum Guidance — embedding climate change learning into ongoing national textbook reform processes.

🌍
Green Curriculum

Africa — Multi-Country Consultations

The Greening Curriculum Guidance was developed through national consultations in Algeria, Egypt, Jordan, Lebanon, Eswatini, Zimbabwe, and Qatar — ensuring global relevance while respecting the cultural and ecological contexts of each region.

🌏
Green Curriculum

Asia Pacific — Climate Justice in Island Nations

Island nations in the Asia Pacific — among the most climate-vulnerable in the world — are prioritizing the Greening Curriculum by integrating disaster literacy, climate adaptation, and community resilience into local school programs.

🇦🇪
Green Curriculum

UAE and Qatar — Sustainability in Arid Contexts

The Gulf region integrates the Greening Curriculum within the context of water scarcity, solar energy expansion, and economic transition away from fossil fuels — demonstrating the framework's adaptability across vastly different geographic and economic conditions.

Indonesian Curriculum Analysis
🇮🇩

Indonesia:
Opportunity & Challenge

As the world's largest archipelagic nation with over 17,000 islands, the second-longest coastline, and the heart of the global Coral Triangle — Indonesia has both the greatest need and the greatest opportunity to lead Blue and Green Curriculum integration in Southeast Asia.

Policy Level Analysis

🏛️
Kurikulum Merdeka and Profil Pelajar Pancasila

The "environmental care" dimension in the Profil Pelajar Pancasila directly aligns with Greening Curriculum principles. The project-based learning component (P5) opens authentic space for Blue and Green integration in everyday teaching.

🚢
Poros Maritim Dunia (Global Maritime Axis)

Indonesia's national maritime vision has not yet been systematically translated into ocean literacy content across formal school curricula — a significant gap between policy ambition and educational reality.

♻️
Adiwiyata Program (Green School Initiative)

This government-run program is a concrete national example of Greening Curriculum in action — schools earn certification by adopting environmentally friendly practices aligned with UNESCO green school quality standards.

Science Curriculum Analysis

IPA and IPAS subjects contain ecosystems and biodiversity as entry points for both Blue and Green Curriculum integration — but ocean content remains significantly underrepresented compared to terrestrial topics.

P5 Theme "Gaya Hidup Berkelanjutan" (Sustainable Lifestyle) provides a concrete, structured vehicle for Blue and Green Curriculum through authentic student-driven projects across all grade levels.

Local maritime knowledge (kearifan lokal) — from Bugis seafarers to Bajo sea nomads — can authentically enrich Blue Curriculum with multi-perspective, culturally grounded ocean understanding.

Climate change in senior high school science is still framed as an abstract global phenomenon rather than a local, actionable issue relevant to students in coastal and agricultural communities.

Theory of Change

From Awareness
to Action

UNESCO's Blue Curriculum proposes a four-part matrix for designing curriculum rich in ocean content — a clear pathway from knowledge to transformative action.

🧠

Knowledge and Awareness

Problem-based, action-based, and wonder-based learning gives young people a foundation to engage with and debate ocean and climate topics critically.

🌱

Nature-Connectedness

Time spent learning in and about nature strengthens positive attitudes and emotional connection with the marine and natural environment.

💡

Values and Attitudes

Developed alongside peers and teachers, these values guide students to make informed and sustainable choices and positively influence their communities.

⚡

Competences and Action

Skills and agency that empower students to take collective sustainable action for the well-being of both their local communities and the global ocean.

Implementation in Education

Bringing It Into
the Classroom

Both frameworks can be implemented right now — in lesson plans, school projects, and community actions. Here is how teachers, students, and citizens can start today.

Sample Lesson Plan

"Our Coastal Classroom" — Grades 7 to 9 Science (IPA/IPAS) — Blue and Green Integration
1
Wonder Phase — Spark Curiosity

Show satellite images of coral bleaching in Indonesian waters. Ask students: "What do you see? What might have caused this?" Activate prior knowledge and natural curiosity before any direct instruction.

2
Exploration Phase — Fieldwork or Virtual Reality

If near the coast: visit a beach or mangrove area. If inland: use virtual 360-degree ocean tours. Students collect real data on species variety, plastic waste density, water quality, or mangrove coverage.

3
Connection Phase — Local to Global

Link local findings to global data. How does Indonesia's plastic waste affect Pacific Ocean health? How do rising sea surface temperatures affect the catch of local fishermen communities?

4
Action Phase — Student Campaign (P5 Project)

Students design a local awareness campaign: social media content, a school pledge wall, a letter to local government officials, or organizing a community coastal clean-up day with family involvement.

👩‍🏫

For Teachers

  • Integrate ocean and climate topics into existing IPA, Geography, and Social Studies lessons
  • Use P5 projects with the "Sustainable Lifestyle" theme for Blue and Green curriculum activities
  • Connect with NGOs like WWF Indonesia, RARE, and Reef Check for free classroom resources
  • Pursue Adiwiyata school certification to formalize environmentally friendly school practices
  • Use the UNESCO Ocean Literacy Portal for freely available ready-to-use teaching materials
🎒

For Students

  • Start or join a school environmental club focused on ocean or climate action campaigns
  • Document local environmental issues through photography, video, or written storytelling
  • Join citizen science projects — coastal clean-ups, species surveys, local weather monitoring
  • Educate your family on sustainable seafood choices and reducing single-use plastic at home
🌍

For Citizens and Communities

  • Support local schools on their Adiwiyata certification journey through volunteer time or donations
  • Share traditional maritime knowledge (kearifan lokal) directly with nearby school communities
  • Advocate with local government for dedicated environmental education budget allocations
  • Help build community-school partnerships for ongoing local environmental monitoring programs
